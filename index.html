General Instructions:

  1. The number, type and pattern of questions, as well as sequence and timing of sections in the Mock Exam are only indicative and these are subject to variations from year to year as decided by the CAT authorities.
  2. The test has 3 (three) sections. The total duration of the test is 120 minutes. PwD candidates will have 13 minutes and 20 seconds extra time for each section.
  3. The time allotted to each section is 40 minutes (53 minutes and 20 seconds for PwD candidates). As soon as you start answering a section, the clock (displayed on the top right corner of the screen) will start. On completion of 40 minutes, the clock will stop, the particular section will be locked, and the responses to questions in that section will be auto-submitted. You will then need to move to the next section and start answering the next set of questions. The same process will be repeated for the other two sections. On submission of all three sections, a summary of your answers will be displayed on your screen.

    For PwD candidates the process will be the same as above. The only exception is that, for each section, the PwD candidate will have 53 minutes and 20 seconds, with an option for submitting the answers of a section at any point of time after the completion of 40 minutes. Thus, PwD candidates will have the option to complete the entire test with all three sections within a maximum of 160 minutes.

  4. You will be allowed to leave the test hall only after a minimum of 120 minutes.
  5. Your time will be set and synchronized to the server clock. The Countdown Timer at the top right corner of your screen will display the remaining time available to you to complete the currently active section. When the timer reaches zero, the test for that section will automatically end.

    PwD candidates will be allowed to complete a section at any point of time between duration of 40 minutes and 53 minutes and 20 seconds by clicking on the 'Submit' button. After 53 minutes and 20 seconds, however, the test for the section will automatically end.

  6. The question paper will have a mix of Multiple Choice Question (MCQ) type with options and Non-MCQ type.
  7. A writing pad will be provided to the candidates for rough work, which will have to be returned after the test. Please note that only one writing pad will be provided to you. Candidates should clearly write their name and registration number on the writing pad.
  8. An on-screen calculator will be provided, which can be used for computing. You will not be allowed to use any other calculator, computing machine, or device.
  9. The question palette displayed on the right side of the screen will show the status of each question with the help of one of the following symbols:
    S. No.Question Number with StatusMeaning
    A1You have not visited the question yet.
    B3You have visited the question but have not answered it.
    C5You have answered the question but have not flagged it as Marked for Review.
    D7You have not answered the question but have flagged it as Marked for Review.
    E*0☰You have answered the question as well as flagged it as Marked for Review.

    *Answers to all questions flagged as 'Marked for Review' (Serial No. E) will be automatically considered as submitted for evaluation at the end of the time allotted for that section.

  10. You can click on the " < " arrow which appears to the left of the question palette to collapse the question palette and maximize the window. To view the question palette again, you can click on the " > " which appears on the right side of the computer console. Please note that you may have to scroll down to view the full question and options in some cases.
  11. To answer a question, you will need to do the following:
    1. Click on the question number in the question palette to go to that question directly.
    2. Select an answer for an MCQ by clicking on the radio button ( ) placed just before the choice.

      For a Non-MCQ, enter only a whole number as the answer in the space provided on the screen using the on-screen keyboard. For example, if the correct answer to a Non-MCQ is 50, then enter ONLY 50 and NOT 50.0 or 050 etc.

    3. Click on 'Save & Next' to save your answer for the current question and then go to the next question.

      Alternatively, you may click on 'Mark for Review & Next' to save your answer for the current question and mark it for your review at any time before the completion of the section, and then move to the next question.

      Caution: Your answer for the current question will not be saved, if you navigate directly to another question by clicking on a question number and not click 'Save & Next' or 'Mark for Review & Next' button.

    4. You will be able to view all the questions of a section by clicking on the 'Question Paper' button. This feature is provided for you to see the entire question paper of a particular section.
  12. Procedure for changing your response to a question:
    1. To deselect your chosen answer, click on the question number on the question palette and click on the 'Clear Response' button.
    2. To change your chosen answer, click on the radio button corresponding to another option.
    3. To save your changed answer, you must click on the 'Save & Next' or 'Mark for Review & Next' button.
  13. Navigating through Sections:
    1. The test has three sections administered in the following order: I. Verbal Ability and Reading Comprehension (VARC), II. Data Interpretation and Logical Reasoning (DILR), and III. Quantitative Ability (QA). The names of the three sections are displayed on the top bar of the screen. The section you are currently viewing is highlighted.
    2. From any section, you will be able to move to the next section only after completing a minimum of 40 minutes, i.e., after the time allocated to that section for non-PwD candidates.
    3. PwD candidates with blindness and low vision (or VI candidates) will have the screen magnification option enabled by default and will find two magnifying glass icons at the top of the screen. You can click on the zoom-in icon to zoom-in and click on the zoom-out icon to zoom out the question. Zoom is enabled at 2 levels from the default view, which are:
      1. 16x Pixel – Default View

      3. 24x Pixel – Level 2

Passage

The paradox of modern democracy is that it has simultaneously expanded the scope of popular participation and narrowed the range of meaningful choice. Over the past century, universal suffrage has extended the franchise to virtually every adult citizen in democratic nations, yet the actual influence of individual voters on policy outcomes has arguably diminished as governance has grown more complex and technocratic.

Political theorists have long grappled with what Robert Dahl called the problem of scale: as democratic units grow larger, direct participation becomes impractical, requiring the delegation of authority to representatives who inevitably develop interests and expertise distinct from their constituents. This delegation creates the principal-agent problem familiar to economists — the agent (elected official) may pursue goals that diverge from those of the principal (the voter).

Contemporary democracies have attempted to manage this tension through institutional design: independent judiciaries, regulatory agencies, central banks, and international bodies that insulate certain decisions from electoral pressure. Proponents argue that such arrangements protect minority rights and ensure technocratic competence; critics contend that they hollow out democratic accountability by placing key decisions beyond the reach of popular will.

The rise of populist movements across the globe can be read, in part, as a reaction to this perceived democratic deficit. When citizens feel that formal electoral mechanisms no longer translate their preferences into policy, they may turn to charismatic outsiders who promise to dismantle the insulating layers and restore unmediated popular sovereignty. Whether such movements represent a revitalisation of democracy or a threat to its liberal foundations remains deeply contested.

The tension between democratic ideals and technocratic governance is not new. From the Platonic critique of mob rule to Schumpeter's minimalist conception of democracy as elite competition, political thinkers have questioned whether ordinary citizens possess the knowledge and deliberative capacity to govern complex modern societies. What is new is the scale of institutional insulation that has developed since the mid-twentieth century.

International institutions such as the World Trade Organization, the International Monetary Fund, and increasingly the European Union have transferred substantial policy authority to arenas that are, by design, insulated from direct democratic control. Decisions affecting tariffs, currency, fiscal policy, and migration are shaped in forums where elected representatives play only a marginal role, and where technical expertise carries more weight than popular mandate.

Defenders of this arrangement point to the failures of purely majoritarian systems: the susceptibility to demagoguery, the short-termism induced by electoral cycles, and the well-documented tendency of voters to favour policies that benefit themselves in the immediate term at the expense of future generations or of minorities. Independent central banks, for example, were deliberately designed to resist the inflationary temptation that governments face when elections are near.

Critics, however, argue that the price of this insulation has been the erosion of political agency. When voters cannot meaningfully influence the decisions that most affect their lives — because those decisions have been delegated to technocrats, bound by international treaty, or shielded by constitutional entrenchment — the formal machinery of democracy becomes a kind of theatre. The ballot retains its symbolic power while losing its practical efficacy.

This disjunction between formal democracy and substantive self-governance is felt most acutely by those at the economic margins, for whom abstract commitments to procedural fairness offer little comfort. The communities that have experienced deindustrialisation, wage stagnation, and the withdrawal of public services have little reason to feel represented by institutions that prioritise macroeconomic stability and investor confidence over local livelihoods.

Whether democracy can reinvent itself to close this gap — by deepening participation at the local level, by redesigning supranational institutions to be more accountable, or by recovering a richer vocabulary of public deliberation — remains the central political challenge of the twenty-first century. The answer will depend not only on institutional engineering but on whether citizens can be persuaded that collective self-governance is worth the effort it requires.

The digital revolution has introduced a new and unresolved dimension to these debates. On the one hand, the internet and social media have dramatically lowered the barriers to political organisation, enabling citizens to coordinate across geographic and social boundaries in ways that were previously impossible. Movements such as the Arab Spring, Occupy Wall Street, and more recently the global climate strikes have demonstrated the mobilising power of networked communication.

On the other hand, the same platforms that empower citizens also concentrate extraordinary influence in the hands of a small number of private corporations. Algorithmic curation shapes what citizens see and believe; micro-targeted advertising allows well-funded interests to tailor political messages with unprecedented precision; and the architecture of engagement — designed to maximise time-on-platform rather than to foster deliberation — tends to reward outrage over nuance.

Political scientists have begun to document what some call the paradox of hyper-connectivity: that an increase in the quantity of political communication has been accompanied by a decline in its quality. Echo chambers and filter bubbles reinforce existing beliefs rather than challenging them; misinformation spreads faster than corrections; and the sheer volume of information makes it increasingly difficult for citizens to distinguish credible sources from unreliable ones.

Responses to this crisis of information have been varied and, so far, inadequate. Platform companies have introduced content moderation policies, but these are applied inconsistently and are often perceived as politically biased. Governments have passed legislation requiring greater transparency in political advertising, but enforcement remains patchy and the pace of regulatory change lags far behind the pace of technological development.

Some theorists argue that the solution lies in redesigning the platforms themselves — replacing engagement-maximising algorithms with ones that prioritise accuracy, diversity of viewpoint, and constructive dialogue. Others contend that platform regulation alone cannot restore democratic health, and that deeper reforms to political education, media literacy, and the funding of public interest journalism are equally necessary.

At a deeper level, the crisis of democracy may reflect a crisis of trust — not only in institutions, but in the possibility of shared truth. When citizens inhabit different informational universes, democratic deliberation, which presupposes at least a minimal common factual basis, becomes extraordinarily difficult. Rebuilding that common epistemic ground is perhaps the most urgent and least tractable challenge facing democratic societies today.
